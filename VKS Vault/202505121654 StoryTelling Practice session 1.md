Matthew Dicks, in his book Storyworthy: Engage, Teach, Persuade, and Change Your Life through the Power of Storytelling, emphasizes crafting personal stories around a "five-second moment" of transformation or realization, using techniques like "Homework for Life" to identify storyworthy moments from everyday experiences. Below are a few storytelling situation examples inspired by Dicks' approach, designed to help you craft compelling narratives. Each situation includes a brief description and a potential five-second moment to anchor your story. You can choose one to develop into a full story.

1. A Moment of Unexpected Kindness

- Situation: You're stranded at a gas station late at night, out of gas and money, feeling defeated after a tough day. A stranger approaches, offers to fill your tank, and disappears before you can thank them properly.
    
- Five-Second Moment: The realization that small acts of kindness can restore your faith in humanity, shifting your perspective from despair to hope.
    
- Story Crafting Tips (from Dicks):
    
    - Start near the end: Begin with you sitting in your car, staring at the empty gas gauge, to hook the audience.
        
    - Use "but" and "therefore" to connect scenes (e.g., "I thought I was stuck, but a stranger appeared, therefore I felt a spark of hope").
        
    - Ground the story in a specific location (the dimly lit gas station, the smell of fuel) to create a "cinema of the mind."
        
    - Reflect change: Show how you started cynical but ended hopeful.
        

Prompt for You: Write a story about this gas station encounter, focusing on the moment your perspective shifts. Describe the setting vividly and show your emotional journey.

2. A Childhood Lesson That Stuck

- Situation: As a kid, you tried to impress your friends by jumping off a high diving board, only to freeze at the top, terrified. Your parent or coach coaxed you down, teaching you about courage or knowing your limits.
    
- Five-Second Moment: The realization that bravery isn’t always about jumping, but sometimes about admitting fear and choosing what’s right for you.
    
- Story Crafting Tips (from Dicks):
    
    - Use the "Dinner Test": Tell this story as you would to a friend over beers, keeping it authentic and conversational.
        
    - Incorporate stakes: Highlight the pressure from friends watching and your internal fear to make the audience care.
        
    - End on heart, not humor: Conclude with the emotional weight of the lesson, not a punchline.
        
    - Use sensory details: Describe the rough texture of the diving board, the chlorine smell, or the crowd’s murmurs.
        

Prompt for You: Craft a story about this diving board moment, showing how you changed from seeking approval to valuing self-awareness. Make the scene vivid and personal.

3. A Workplace Misstep Turned Triumph

- Situation: You gave a presentation at work that went horribly wrong—maybe you forgot your lines or the tech failed. Afterward, a colleague’s feedback or an unexpected opportunity (like a client appreciating your honesty) turned the failure into a growth moment.
    
- Five-Second Moment: The realization that failure can be a stepping stone to growth, shifting your view from embarrassment to confidence.
    
- Story Crafting Tips (from Dicks):
    
    - Find the small moment: Don’t focus on the big failure but on the subtle shift in perspective (e.g., a colleague’s quiet encouragement).
        
    - Use "breadcrumbs": Hint at the coming redemption early (e.g., “I noticed my colleague watching me closely, but I didn’t know why yet”) to build anticipation.
        
    - Show vulnerability: Be honest about your embarrassment to connect with the audience.
        
    - Apply the "but-and-therefore" rule: “I bombed the presentation, but a client reached out, therefore I saw failure differently.”
        

Prompt for You: Write a story about this workplace blunder, focusing on the moment you realized failure wasn’t the end. Use specific details about the setting and your emotions.

4. A Family Moment of Connection

- Situation: You’re playing with your child, sibling, or relative, and they say or do something simple but profound—like a child pointing out something you’ve overlooked in your busy adult life. It makes you pause and rethink your priorities.
    
- Five-Second Moment: The realization that small, everyday interactions hold deep meaning, shifting you from distraction to presence.
    
- Story Crafting Tips (from Dicks):
    
    - Focus on the tiny moment: The story isn’t about the whole day but the specific instant of connection (e.g., your child’s words).
        
    - Use present tense for key moments: Describe the five-second moment in present tense to pull the audience in (e.g., “She looks at me and says…”).
        
    - Create stakes: Show what you were preoccupied with (work stress, deadlines) to highlight the contrast with the moment of clarity.
        
    - Keep it personal: Tell your story, not the child’s, focusing on your transformation.
        

Prompt for You: Craft a story about this family interaction, emphasizing the moment you became more present. Use vivid locations and show your internal change.

How to Craft Your Story (Dicks’ Key Principles)

- Homework for Life: Reflect on your day to find storyworthy moments. For any of these situations, think of a real-life parallel from your own experience to make the story authentic.
    
- Five-Second Moment: Ensure your story hinges on a moment of change or realization, even if it’s subtle.
    
- Start at the End: Begin as close to the transformation as possible, then flashback to show the journey.
    
- Cinema of the Mind: Always anchor scenes in a physical location to make the story vivid.
    
- Vulnerability and Authenticity: Share your emotions and struggles to connect with the audience.
