# Gluten

  
Gluten is primarily found in grains like:

  

- Wheat: This includes all varieties such as spelt, kamut, farro, and durum, as well as products like bread, pasta, couscous, and bulgur.
    
- Barley: Found in malt, food coloring, soups, beer (unless it's gluten-free), and some vinegars.
    
- Rye: Rye bread, rye beer, and some rye whiskeys contain gluten.
    
- Triticale: A cross between wheat and rye, used in some flours and breads.
    

  
Gluten can also be present in:

  

- Processed Foods: Many processed foods use wheat flour or wheat starch as a thickener, stabilizer, or filler. This includes items like:
    
    - Sauces, gravies, and dressings
        
    - Soups and broths
        
    - Breaded or battered foods
        
    - Breakfast cereals
        
    - Snack foods (like crackers, chips, pretzels)
        
- Baked Goods: All traditional baking unless specified as gluten-free:
    
    - Cakes, cookies, pies, pastries
        
    - Breads, rolls, bagels, muffins
        
- Beer and Other Alcoholic Beverages: Most traditional beers are made from barley or wheat, although there are gluten-free beers available. Some liquors might also use grains containing gluten in their production.
    
- Medications and Supplements: Some might use gluten as a binding agent.
    
- Cosmetics: Occasionally, gluten might be in cosmetics like lip balms or lipstick, though this is less common and generally not a concern unless ingested.
    

  

When looking to avoid gluten, it's important to check labels for ingredients like wheat, barley, rye, malt, or modified food starch, and be aware of cross-contamination in manufacturing processes. Foods labeled as "gluten-free" are regulated to contain less than 20 parts per million of gluten in many countries, ensuring they are safe for those with celiac disease or gluten sensitivities.


# Gluten Rich Foods in INDIA: 

  
In India, several foods are rich in gluten due to the widespread use of wheat and other gluten-containing grains. Here's a list of some common gluten-rich foods:

  

- Roti/Chapati: Made from whole wheat flour (atta).
    
- Naan: Leavened flatbread made from refined wheat flour (maida).
    
- Paratha: Layered flatbread, often stuffed, made from wheat flour.
    
- Puri: Deep-fried bread made from wheat flour.
    
- Bhatura: Deep-fried leavened bread made from maida.
    
- Kulcha: Similar to naan but often stuffed with fillings like potato or paneer.
    
- Bread: Various types including white, brown, or multigrain, all usually containing wheat.
    
- Biscuits/Cookies: Most are made with wheat flour unless specified gluten-free.
    
- Pasta: Includes noodles, macaroni, and other pasta shapes, typically made from wheat.
    
- Sevian (Vermicelli): Often used in sweet dishes like seviyan kheer or savory dishes like upma.
    
- Baked Goods: Cakes, pastries, muffins, and other bakery items which typically use wheat flour.
    
- Samosa and Kachori: The outer shell is usually made from wheat flour.
    
- Pakoras: The batter for these fritters sometimes includes wheat flour for binding.
    

  

Additionally, many processed foods in India might contain gluten unless they are explicitly labeled as gluten-free. This includes snacks like chips, flavored namkeens, and some instant mixes for snacks or meals.

  

If you or someone you know is looking to avoid gluten, it's crucial to check labels, ask about ingredients in restaurants, and consider traditional alternatives like rice, millet (bajra), sorghum (jowar), or pulses which are naturally gluten-free. Remember, even foods like spices or condiments might have gluten if they contain added thickeners or stabilizers.

# South Asian Gluten Rich Foods 

Here's a breakdown of what typically contains gluten and what are considered gluten-free options within a Desi (South Asian) diet:

Foods That Contain Gluten in a Desi Diet:

- Wheat Products:
    
    - Roti/Chapati: Made from whole wheat flour (atta).
        
    - Paratha: Layered flatbread, also made from wheat flour.
        
    - Naan: A leavened flatbread made with wheat flour.
        
    - Puri: Deep-fried bread, commonly made from wheat flour.
        
    - Bhatura: Similar to puri but larger, made with wheat flour and often fermented.
        
- Semolina (Sooji/Rawa): Used in dishes like upma, halwa, and some types of idli or dosa.
    
- Baked Goods:
    
    - Any cakes, cookies, or pastries made with maida (refined wheat flour).
        
- Pasta and Noodles: Including instant noodles which might contain wheat.
    
- Processed Foods: Many snacks like samosas, pakoras, and certain types of biscuits might have wheat flour.
    


Gluten-Free Foods in a Desi Diet:

- Rice:
    
    - Common in forms like basmati or sona masuri, used in dishes like biryani, pulao, or simple rice dishes.
        
- Alternative Flours:
    
    - Bajra (Pearl Millet) - Used for rotis or bhakri.
        
    - Jowar (Sorghum) - Similar use as bajra.
        
    - Ragi (Finger Millet) - Good for porridge or flatbreads.
        
    - Rice Flour - Used for making idli, dosa, or some sweets.
        
- Pulses and Lentils:
    
    - Dal of all kinds (chana, moong, urad, masoor, etc.) are naturally gluten-free.
        
- Vegetables: All vegetables are gluten-free unless prepared with ingredients that contain gluten.
    
- Meats, Fish, and Eggs: Naturally gluten-free, but watch out for marinades or sauces that might include gluten.
    
- Dairy: Milk, paneer, ghee, and yogurt are all gluten-free.
    
- Spices: Most pure spices are gluten-free, but always check for cross-contamination or added fillers in spice mixes.
    
- Fruits and Nuts: All fruits and nuts are gluten-free.
    


Additional Tips:

- Cross-Contamination: Be cautious about cross-contamination in kitchen tools, especially when preparing both gluten and gluten-free foods.
    
- Label Reading: In India, some packaged foods might not clearly state if they contain gluten. Look for wheat, barley, or rye in ingredients lists.
    
- Eating Out: Communicate clearly if you need gluten-free options at restaurants.
    

  

This list should help you navigate a gluten-free diet within a Desi context. Remember, individual sensitivities can vary, so it might be useful to consult with a nutritionist or dietician for personalized advice, especially if you're dealing with celiac disease or gluten sensitivity.

# How to know if you're gut does not accept Gluten? 

Your body might not accept gluten if you experience certain symptoms after consuming gluten-containing foods like wheat, barley, and rye. Gluten intolerance, sensitivity, or celiac disease can manifest in various ways, including:

---

### **Common Symptoms of Gluten Intolerance/Sensitivity:**

1. **Digestive Issues:**
    
    - Bloating, gas, or abdominal pain after eating gluten-containing foods.
    - Diarrhea or constipation.
    - Nausea.
2. **Fatigue and Lethargy:**
    
    - Feeling unusually tired, especially after meals containing gluten.
3. **Skin Issues:**
    
    - Rashes or itchy skin, commonly linked to dermatitis herpetiformis (specific to celiac disease).
    - Dry or flaky skin.
4. **Headaches or Migraines:**
    
    - Recurring headaches, often triggered by gluten-rich meals.
5. **Joint and Muscle Pain:**
    
    - Unexplained aches, stiffness, or inflammation in joints.
6. **Neurological Symptoms:**
    
    - Brain fog, difficulty concentrating, or mood swings.
    - Numbness or tingling in the extremities (hands and feet).
7. **Unexplained Weight Changes:**
    
    - Unintentional weight loss or difficulty gaining weight, often linked to malabsorption.
8. **Iron-Deficiency Anemia:**
    
    - Persistent anemia that doesn’t respond well to iron supplements.
9. **Autoimmune Disorders:**
    
    - If you have an autoimmune condition (e.g., thyroid disorders, Type 1 diabetes), you might be more likely to have gluten sensitivity or celiac disease.

---

### **Steps to Confirm Gluten Intolerance:**

1. **Track Your Symptoms:**
    
    - Keep a food and symptom diary to identify patterns.
2. **Elimination Diet:**
    
    - Avoid gluten for 4–6 weeks to see if symptoms improve, then reintroduce it to confirm.
3. **Medical Testing:**
    
    - Consult a healthcare professional for:
        - **Celiac Panel Blood Test**: Checks for antibodies indicating celiac disease.
        - **Endoscopy/Biopsy**: Confirms intestinal damage in celiac cases.
        - **Genetic Testing**: Identifies predisposition to celiac disease (HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 genes).
4. **Professional Diagnosis:**
    
    - Gluten sensitivity is often diagnosed by ruling out celiac disease and wheat allergy.

---

If you suspect gluten intolerance, consider seeking advice from a gastroenterologist or dietitian. Early identification can prevent complications and improve your quality of life.
