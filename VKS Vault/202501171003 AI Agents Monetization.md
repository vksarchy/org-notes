    - Organizational Silos: There's often a lack of unified approach within enterprises, leading to shadow IT and governance issues.

    - Agentic Workflow: AI agents are seen as tools that perform tasks autonomously but require careful definition and integration into existing workflows.
        
    - Agents vs. Microservices: There's a parallel drawn between the evolution from monolithic to microservices architecture in software and the current evolution towards agentic systems in AI, suggesting that many of the same challenges (like discoverability and collaboration) persist.

    - Customer Support: Initial applications of AI agents are in customer service, reducing response times dramatically.
        
    - Workflow Integration: The real power of AI agents lies in integrating them into business workflows like marketing, sales, fraud detection, and investment research, where they can automate routine tasks but still require human oversight for accuracy.
        
4. AI Development and Evolution:
    
    - Rapid Tech Evolution: AI technology, particularly LLMs, is evolving rapidly, which complicates the ROI calculation and strategic planning for enterprises.
        
    - Need for Continuous Learning: Keeping up with AI developments requires ongoing education and engagement with both internal research and industry trends.

- Testing and Validation: Establish robust testing frameworks to assess AI applications' accuracy and reliability before production.
    
- Integration with Existing Systems: Plan for how AI can fit into current workflows, emphasizing governance to manage shadow IT.
    
- ROI Framework: Develop a dynamic framework for measuring ROI that can adapt to evolving technology and business needs.
